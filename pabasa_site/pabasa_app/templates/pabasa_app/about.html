Reading Checks Take Time

Why PABASA was built to support faster, more structured, and more useful reading assessment workflows for teachers.

Dona Palacios PABASA Research Notes 5 min read

Reading is one of the most important skills teachers assess, as it forms the foundation for learning across all subjects. Effective and efficient methods for evaluating students are more crucial than ever, particularly given the increasing emphasis on literacy. To accurately monitor and evaluate a student's reading ability, teachers must do more than simply determine whether a student can read words correctly. They must observe reading fluency, identify pronunciation and decoding errors, assess comprehension and vocabulary skills, and provide meaningful feedback. In addition, teachers often document observations and analyze assessment results to identify learning gaps and determine appropriate instructional interventions. As a result, reading assessments require significant time and attention (Davis, 2024).

During the researcher's data-gathering process, it was revealed that schools often conduct reading assessments manually, requiring teachers to evaluate students individually to ensure accuracy and reliability. However, manual assessment is inherently time-consuming because teachers must check, evaluate, and compare student performance, which becomes increasingly difficult to manage in large classrooms (Norova & Asrorov, 2021). While traditional assessment methods allow teachers to handle multiple learners within limited time, they still involve substantial effort in scoring and documentation, contributing to longer evaluation periods (Davis, 2024).

The assessment process becomes even more demanding when teachers conduct progress monitoring, record student performance, and interpret results to guide instructional decisions. Beyond simple scoring, teachers must analyze student strengths and weaknesses to ensure accurate evaluation of reading development (Norova & Asrorov, 2021). These responsibilities increase workload and limit the number of students that can be assessed within a given time frame (Davis, 2024).

Unlike automated systems, reading evaluations are typically conducted manually, requiring teachers to listen, observe, and document performance for each student. This repetitive process makes reading checks particularly time-intensive in classrooms with large class sizes. In addition, alternative or performance-based assessments require even more detailed judgment and structured criteria, further increasing the time needed for evaluation (Norova & Asrorov, 2021). As teachers balance multiple responsibilities such as lesson planning and classroom management, reading assessment becomes even more demanding, especially when supporting learners who require additional guidance (Extramarks, 2025).

That is why, in PABASA, we aim to address the time-intensive nature of reading checks by supporting a more efficient and structured assessment process for teachers. Our solution allows teachers to evaluate up to 10 students more quickly without sacrificing accuracy, using tools that track fluency and decoding in real time. By automating data recording and providing instant insights, PABASA helps teachers identify learning gaps, monitor student progress, and plan interventions more effectively - saving valuable classroom time while maintaining high-quality assessments.

Make the Workflow Focused

How structured reading levels and consistent assessment steps help teachers make clearer instructional decisions.

Dona Palacios PABASA Research Notes 6 min read

Reading assessment is often manual and fragmented, requiring teachers to evaluate multiple skills such as fluency, decoding, and comprehension within a single evaluation process (National Reading Panel, 2000; Snow, 2002). Because reading is a multi-component skill, assessment places a high cognitive and interpretive demand on teachers, as they must analyze performance and provide feedback simultaneously (Brookhart, 2013). This makes reading checks both time-intensive and mentally demanding, especially in classrooms with large student populations where individual assessment time is limited (OECD, 2013; Black & Wiliam, 1998).

To improve effectiveness, reading assessment should be built around structured reading levels that reflect students' developmental stages. Research shows that learners progress more effectively when instruction and assessment are aligned with their current skill level rather than delivered through a one-size-fits-all approach (Snow, 2002; National Reading Panel, 2000). Level-based progression ensures that assessment is purposeful and supports gradual skill development instead of random or inconsistent evaluation.

A structured workflow further strengthens the assessment process by reducing decision fatigue and standardizing evaluation steps. When teachers follow a clear sequence of procedures, it improves scoring consistency and reduces variability in judgment. This aligns with research on formative assessment, which emphasizes that structured systems enhance reliability and teacher effectiveness in classroom evaluation (Stiggins, 2005; Brookhart, 2013).

In addition, structured assessment processes improve fairness and reliability in student evaluation. Manual grading can vary due to subjective interpretation, but standardized procedures help ensure more consistent results across different students and classroom conditions (Harlen, 2007). This consistency is essential for accurate tracking of reading development over time.

To address these challenges, PABASA introduces a more focused, structured, and decision-driven assessment process for teachers. By organizing evaluation around reading levels, the system reduces fragmentation in manual assessment and standardizes how student performance is measured. This workflow minimizes teacher cognitive load while improving consistency and fairness. More importantly, it converts assessment results into clear, actionable summaries that support instructional decisions, enabling teachers to quickly identify student progress and determine appropriate next steps in learning.

Support Better Follow-Up

How meaningful insights, timely feedback, and progress monitoring help teachers guide learners after assessment.

Dona Palacios PABASA Research Notes 6 min read

Effective learning does not end when an assessment is completed. Research has consistently shown that feedback has a significant positive impact on student achievement, making follow-up an essential part of the learning process (Wisniewski, Zierer, & Hattie, 2020). One of the most valuable outcomes of our system is its ability to support better follow-up by providing educators with meaningful insights into student performance. Rather than replacing teacher expertise and judgment, the system serves as a tool that helps teachers make more informed decisions about how to support their learners.

By analyzing assessment results, the system can highlight patterns that may otherwise be difficult to identify, especially in larger classes. Teachers can quickly determine which students may need additional guidance, reinforcement, or intervention. Educational analytics research suggests that performance monitoring can help identify at-risk learners early, allowing educators to provide timely support before academic difficulties become more serious (Aulck et al., 2020).

The information generated by the system also enables more personalized feedback. Instead of relying solely on general comments, teachers can tailor their recommendations based on a student's individual strengths and areas for improvement. This personalized approach helps learners better understand their progress and focus on specific skills that need development. The importance of timely feedback is further supported by a Philippine study, which found that technology-based assessments providing prompt feedback improved the performance of struggling Grade 10 mathematics students and positively influenced their learning experience (Avenir et al., 2025).

In addition, the system supports targeted practice and remediation. Educators can use the insights provided to recommend appropriate activities, review materials, or learning strategies that align with each student's needs. Research has shown that assessment data can be used to guide remediation efforts and targeted interventions, helping address learning gaps and improve student achievement (Avenir et al., 2025).

Another important benefit is the ability to monitor progress over time. By comparing assessment results across multiple periods, teachers can evaluate whether interventions are working and make adjustments when necessary. This creates a continuous cycle of assessment, feedback, intervention, and improvement.

Ultimately, the goal is to empower educators with actionable information that strengthens teaching and learning. By providing a clearer starting point for feedback, practice, and intervention, the system helps ensure that every student receives the support needed to achieve academic success.
